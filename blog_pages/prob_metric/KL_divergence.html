For discrete probability distribution P and Q defined on the same probability space $\mathcal{X}$, the KL divergence is written as: $$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \| Q)=\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} P(x) \log \left(\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\right)$$ For continuous probability distribution ..., the KL divergenece is written as: $$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \| Q)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(x) \log \left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right) d x$$ The term $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \| Q)$ is essentially the relative entropy of P with regard to Q.
It is often used to measure the information gain achieved if P would be used instead of Q which is currently used.

Properties

  1. It is always non-negative.
  2. It is invariant under parameter transformations.
  3. It is additive for independent distribution. If $P_1, P_2$ are indp. distributions, and $Q_1, Q_2$ likewise, then: $$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \| Q)=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_1 \| Q_1)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_2 \| Q_2)$$
  4. It is convex in the pair of pmfs $(p, q)$. $$D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left(\lambda p_{1}+(1-\lambda) p_{2} \| \lambda q_{1}+(1-\lambda) q_{2}\right) \leq \lambda D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left(p_{1} \| q_{1}\right)+(1-\lambda) D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left(p_{2} \| q_{2}\right) \text{, for $0 \leq \lambda \leq 1$}$$
